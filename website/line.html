The Production Line

The KAMAR SODA simulates the production of a 500 ml carbonated soda bottle. The line includes six stations: bottle blowing, soda filling, carbonation, capping, labeling, and quality control with packing.

Station 1 — Bottle Blow

The production process begins with a PET preform, a small plastic tube that contains the material required to form the final bottle. The preforms are heated to the correct temperature before entering the blow moulding station.

Compressed air is injected into the heated preform while it is held inside a mould. The plastic expands until it takes the shape of a 500 ml soda bottle. This process is widely used in the beverage industry because it produces lightweight and durable containers at high speed.

The station verifies that the bottle has been formed correctly before it moves to the filling station. Bottles with structural defects are rejected to prevent production problems further down the line.

Station 2 — Soda Fill

After the bottle has been formed, it is transported to the filling station. This station dispenses the required volume of soda into the bottle using a controlled filling system.

Accurate filling is critical because underfilled bottles fail quality requirements while overfilled bottles increase production costs. Sensors continuously monitor the filling process to ensure consistency.

In the simulation, a bottle may occasionally fail the fill-level inspection. These bottles are marked as defective and removed from the production line.

Station 3 — Carbonation

Carbonation is the process of dissolving carbon dioxide gas into the beverage. This stage gives the product its characteristic sparkling effect and improves the overall drinking experience.

The carbonation station controls pressure and gas flow to achieve the desired carbonation level. Maintaining a consistent carbonation profile is essential for product quality and customer satisfaction.

If the carbonation level falls below the required specification, the bottle is classified as defective and is removed from the line during quality control.

Station 4 — Cap On

The capping station seals the bottle immediately after filling and carbonation. A mechanical system places a cap on each bottle and tightens it to a predefined torque value.

Proper sealing prevents product leakage and protects the beverage from contamination. The station also ensures that carbonation remains trapped inside the bottle until consumption.

Missing or loose caps are detected through inspection checks. Any bottle that fails these checks is automatically rejected.

Station 5 — Label

The labeling station applies the product label to the outside of the bottle. The label contains branding information, nutritional details, product identification, and manufacturing data.

Modern production systems use automated label applicators to maintain consistent positioning and appearance. Accurate label placement is important for both regulatory compliance and product presentation.

Bottles with damaged or missing labels are flagged as defective because they do not meet packaging standards.

Station 6 — QC & Pack

The final stage combines quality control and packaging operations. The station inspects each bottle to verify fill level, carbonation quality, cap placement, and label condition.

Only bottles that pass every inspection are accepted as finished products. Rejected bottles are recorded as defects and removed from the production flow.

Accepted bottles are packed into cartons and prepared for shipment to distribution centers and retail locations. This station represents the final checkpoint before the product leaves the manufacturing facility.
